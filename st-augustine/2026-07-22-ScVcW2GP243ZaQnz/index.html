Subject: 🏗️ St. Johns projects could fast-track 5,400 acres

Preview text: Inside: $4M in SR 16 upgrades, 37 shelter beds, Ice Plant cocktails, and The Black Keys.

In today's Digest:

  • 🏗️ Six development requests could fast-track 5,400 acres across St. Johns County
  • 🚕 New tour rules cap small franchises and pedicab operators at 15 each
  • 🚦 A $4 million SR 16 safety project is due by late September
  • 🏠 Lincolnville's new family shelter adds 37 beds in nine private rooms
  • 🚧 Four weeks of paving work has started across four city neighborhoods

🫧 Cocktails inside St. Augustine's 1920s ice factory

Located at 110 Riberia Street, Ice Plant serves farm-to-table food and craft cocktails inside the city's restored 1920s ice factory. The brick-and-wood room opens for lunch, dinner, and weekend brunch.

The building once made ice for St. Augustine's fishing industry, and the bar keeps that history in view with three types of ice makers. Drinks such as the Flagler Billionaire and Florida Mule add local references, while the kitchen turns out hand-cut fries, sandwiches, and local seafood.

🏗️ Six projects could fast-track 5,400 acres

The Gist: A new state law makes it easier for landowners to seek agricultural-enclave status, potentially bypassing normal rezoning and comprehensive-plan reviews. St. Johns County has all six applications filed statewide, covering 5,400 acres, and Commissioner Ann Taylor asked the board to consider outside counsel and a possible injunction.

Why It Matters: Residents say roads, schools and stormwater systems could bear the consequences while the county has far less control over the usual development review process.

What's Next: All six applications are scheduled to reach the County Commission on August 18.

Read more here.

🚕 St. Augustine caps tour operators and pedicabs

The Gist: City commissioners approved a 15-franchisee cap for tour companies carrying 11 passengers or fewer, with up to two vehicles per franchise, plus a cap of 15 pedicab operators with two pedicabs each. Drivers will need background checks and Florida licenses, while updated carriage-horse rules and impoundment penalties strengthen enforcement.

Why It Matters: The rules reshape a visible piece of downtown transportation and tourism while putting new limits on congestion, operator growth and animal-welfare practices.

What's Next: The ordinances take effect July 23, with warnings through September 30 and penalties beginning October 1.

Read more here.

🚦 $4 million SR 16 safety upgrades due this fall

The Gist: St. Johns County says a project costing more than $4 million will add traffic signals at two intersections, protected pedestrian crosswalks, controlled-turn median openings, new pavement markings and roadway changes near SR 16 and Stratton Boulevard. The timetable drew fresh attention after a July 18 crash killed a 68-year-old driver and seriously injured a deputy.

Why It Matters: Drivers and cyclists currently navigate high-speed traffic and unprotected left turns at an intersection residents have repeatedly called dangerous.

What's Next: The county expects the intersection improvements to be finished in late September.

Read more here.

🏠 Goff Family Shelter adds 37 beds in Lincolnville

The Gist: The St. Augustine Society and the city have completed the Goff Family Shelter at 64 Washington Street, restoring a building damaged by Hurricanes Matthew and Irma with federal Community Development Block Grant support. Its nine private family rooms hold 37 beds, alongside shared spaces, updated utilities, meals and housing-support services.

Why It Matters: The shelter lets parents and children remain together during homelessness while they work toward permanent housing instead of splitting across different facilities.

Read more here.

🚧 Eight city streets enter four-week paving cycle

The Gist: Preferred Materials began milling and resurfacing on Riberia and Orange streets downtown, Fred Waters Way and Nesbit Avenue in West City, East Carver Street and Magnolia Drive in Lighthouse Park, and Inlet Place and Arpieka Avenue in Davis Shores. Streets remain open, but delays are expected and active work zones lose on-street parking from 7 a.m. to 5 p.m. weekdays.

Why It Matters: The work touches four neighborhoods, and vehicles left in marked construction areas can be towed at the owner's expense.

What's Next: The contractor expects to finish within four weeks, weather permitting.
