🍚 Gullah crab rice on Blake Street

Hannibal’s Kitchen

At 16 Blake Street on Charleston’s East Side, Hannibal’s Kitchen has served the Huger family’s soul food since 1985. Crab and shrimp rice anchors a menu of okra soup, fried chicken, pork chops, collard greens, and other Lowcountry staples.

Granddaughters Safiya Grant and Felicity Huger now carry the restaurant into its third generation. Family photos line the unfussy dining room, and the crab and shrimp rice remains the order regulars point newcomers toward.

🚧 Highway 41 Clears 5-4 Vote

🚧 Highway 41 Clears 5-4 Vote

The Gist: Mount Pleasant Town Council voted 5-4 Tuesday to give municipal consent to the nearly $250 million Highway 41 Corridor Improvement Project. The design widens most of the highway to four lanes, keeps a three-lane section through the historic Phillips Community and adds the two-lane Laurel Hill Parkway connector.

Why It Matters: The vote keeps a major congestion project alive while committing the town to a design that residents debated over taxpayer money, historic communities and impacts to Laurel Hill Park.

What's Next: Charleston County plans to finish permits in September and hopes to break ground by the start of 2027.

🏠 Charleston Eyes Eight-Guest Rental Cap

🏠 Charleston Eyes Eight-Guest Rental Cap

The Gist: Charleston's Planning Commission advanced the first major update to the city's short-term-rental ordinance since 2018. The proposal would replace the four-unrelated-adults rule with bedroom-based occupancy limits set by the fire marshal, plus a hard cap of eight guests at any rental.

Why It Matters: Charleston has about 600 licensed short-term rentals in its overlay district, and the update would give operators and neighbors a clearer standard while preserving complaint-driven enforcement. Only 29 rentals are currently approved for more than eight guests.

What's Next: The proposal now goes to Charleston City Council for two readings and additional public comment before any changes can take effect.

🏫 Riverstone Offers a Flexible School Day

🏫 Riverstone Offers a Flexible School Day

The Gist: Riverstone High School, South Carolina's first Second Mile school, will open in North Charleston in August for middle and high school students facing barriers to graduation or seeking an accelerated path. The free public charter school will offer morning and afternoon four-hour sessions, open enrollment with no cap and free bus passes for students who live more than 1.5 miles away.

Why It Matters: The schedule gives students with jobs, family duties or difficult school experiences another route to earn credits and graduate.

What's Next: A community block party with campus tours and teacher meet-and-greets runs Friday, July 17, from 5 to 8 p.m.

🌱 Herbicide Hits Gadsden Creek Plants

🌱 Herbicide Hits Gadsden Creek Plants

The Gist: A Dominion Energy contractor applied glyphosate and imazapyr beneath power lines at Gadsden Creek, killing groundsel, marsh elder and sea ox-eye daisy. Dominion called it routine vegetation management. Charleston officials said the city was not notified, while environmental groups said the low-growing native plants posed no threat to the lines.

Why It Matters: Only four acres remain of the historic creek's wetlands, and conservation groups warn that losing native marsh plants can weaken banks, water quality and habitat for species including shrimp and crabs.

What's Next: The M.A.R.S.H. Project and Friends of Gadsden Creek will hold a species-recording event at the creek on July 25.

🌳 Sol Legare's 200-Year-Old Oak Stays

🌳 Sol Legare's 200-Year-Old Oak Stays

The Gist: A developer withdrew a request to remove a roughly 200-year-old, 40-inch-diameter grand sand oak at 2176 Richardson Road in Sol Legare. The withdrawal followed more than 2,300 petition signatures and 500 public comments, and came after the James Island Public Service District offered another way to provide fire access for two planned residential lots.

Why It Matters: The agreement preserves a landmark tree while allowing the property proposal to continue, showing how public pressure and infrastructure planning can change development outcomes on James Island.

What's Next: Organizers expect the applicant to seek a grand-tree encroachment next, but no filing date has been announced.
